Line Chart:

In this visualization, we explore monthly temperature trends for a chosen state across selected years. For each chosen year, two lines and a scatter plot depict maximum, minimum, and average temperature variations over the months. The y-axis displays temperatures in Celsius, and hovering over each point reveals a tooltip with the precise temperature value and its Fahrenheit conversion.


Radar Chart:

Here, we present three distinct radar charts designed to illustrate monthly temperature trends for a chosen state across selected years. Each radar chart corresponds to the visualization of either maximum, minimum, or average temperatures. Users can hover over individual points to display a tooltip featuring the precise temperature value in Celsius and its conversion to Fahrenheit. The top buttons facilitate the modification of the selected state and the addition/removal of different years, with a maximum of 10 years simultaneously. To enhance clarity when dealing with multiple years on the plot, a legend is provided, associating each line's color with its corresponding year. Moreover, to address potential challenges associated with numerous years, users can click on one or more colors in the legend to isolate their respective years, allowing for a focused visualization on the radar char


Ridge Chart:

Presented here is a ridgeline chart featuring the temperature scale in Celsius along the horizontal axis. This chart displays two distinct data densities, one for minimum temperatures (depicted in blue) and the other for maximum temperatures (highlighted in red). The height and shape of the "ridges" serve as visual indicators of the concentration and distribution of data for a specific year. Within the visualization, two vertical lines in green delineate the minimum and maximum temperature values across all years for the chosen state. Users can utilize the buttons at the top of the page to modify the selected state, as well as add or remove different years, with a maximum limit of 10 years simultaneously.
